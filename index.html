What is stoicism?

stoicism is a practical philosophy focused on living a good life

it teaches us to focus on what we can control, accept what we can't, and stay calm no matter what happens

History of stoicism

Stoicism was founded in Athens, Greece, around the 3rd century BCE by a philosopher named Zeno of Citium. The philosophy was named after the Stoa Poikile, or "Painted Porch," where Zeno and his followers would gather for discussions.

Zeno was influenced by earlier philosophical schools such as Cynicism, which emphasized self-sufficiency and living in accordance with nature. However, Stoicism expanded upon these ideas and developed its own distinctive teachings.

Stoicism gained popularity throughout the Hellenistic period and the Roman Empire, attracting notable followers such as Seneca, Epictetus, and the Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius. It offered practical guidance on how to live a virtuous life, achieve tranquility, and navigate the challenges of

The Stoics believed in living in harmony with nature, cultivating virtues such as wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance, and focusing on what is within one's control while accepting what is not. They also emphasized the importance of rationality, self-discipline, and mindfulness in achieving eudaimonia, or flourishing.

Over time, Stoicism evolved and adapted to different cultural contexts, leaving a lasting legacy that continues to influence philosophy, psychology, and self-help literature to this day.

Stoic virtues

Wisdom: In Stoicism, wisdom involves seeking understanding and insight into the nature of reality and living in accordance with reason. It entails making rational choices and having a clear understanding of what is truly valuable in life.

Courage: Stoic courage involves facing challenges and adversity with resilience and fortitude. It is the ability to act bravely in the face of fear or discomfort, guided by moral principles and a commitment to doing what is right.

Justice: Stoic justice encompasses treating others with fairness, kindness, and respect. It involves acting in accordance with moral principles and fulfilling one's duties and obligations towards others in society.

Temperance: Stoic temperance is the practice of moderation and self-control in all aspects of life. It involves managing desires and impulses, avoiding excess, and maintaining balance and harmony in one's thoughts, emotions, and actions.

Stoic disciplines

Discipline of Desire: Stoicism teaches individuals to properly evaluate their desires and aversions. By distinguishing between what is within their control (internal) and what is not (external), practitioners can focus their efforts on cultivating inner virtues rather than pursuing external outcomes. This discipline encourages embracing what is in accordance with nature and accepting what is not, thereby fostering inner peace and contentment.

Discipline of Action: The discipline of action involves applying Stoic principles to one's behavior and choices. Stoics advocate for acting in accordance with reason, virtue, and moral duty, regardless of external circumstances. This discipline emphasizes practicing virtues such as wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance in everyday life. By aligning actions with Stoic virtues, individuals can lead a more ethical and meaningful existence.

Discipline of Assent: The discipline of assent pertains to how individuals respond to their impressions and perceptions of the world. Stoicism teaches practitioners to critically examine their judgments and reactions, recognizing that many external events are beyond their control. By cultivating a rational and objective perspective, individuals can avoid being swayed by emotions or irrational beliefs. This discipline fosters mental clarity, resilience, and equanimity in the face of adversity.

Stoic techniques to practice

Morning preparation & evening reflection

The Stoic practice of morning preparation and evening reflection is a powerful technique for cultivating self-awareness, resilience, and moral development. Here's how it works:

Morning preparation:

  • Start your day by setting aside a few minutes for reflection and preparation.
  • Begin by reminding yourself of Stoic principles, such as the dichotomy of control and the virtues of wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance.
  • Visualize the challenges you may encounter during the day and mentally prepare yourself to respond to them in accordance with Stoic values.
  • Set intentions for how you want to behave and what virtues you want to embody throughout the day.
  • Practice gratitude by reflecting on things you're thankful for, fostering a positive mindset to approach the day's events.

Evening reflection:

  • At the end of the day, take time to reflect on your experiences and actions.
  • Review your day with honesty and self-awareness, considering how well you lived up to Stoic principles and virtues.
  • Identify any instances where you may have acted in accordance with Stoic values or where you could have done better.

Memento Mori

"Memento Mori" is a Latin phrase that translates to "Remember that you will die." In Stoicism, the memento mori technique serves as a reminder of the impermanence of life and the inevitability of death.

Stoicism teaches that the prospect of death should inspire us to live virtuously and make choices that contribute to our moral development. When faced with difficult decisions, remembering our mortality can help us focus on what is truly important and act in accordance with Stoic virtues.

Amor fati

"Amor Fati" is a Latin phrase meaning "love of fate" or "love of one's fate." In Stoicism, this concept encourages individuals to embrace and accept everything that happens in life, whether positive or negative, as necessary and beneficial for their overall development.

It teaches individuals to fully accept and embrace the present moment, including all its joys and challenges. Rather than resisting or resenting circumstances, Stoics are encouraged to cultivate a deep love and appreciation for whatever unfolds in their lives.

Finally, "amor fati" is rooted in the Stoic belief in living in harmony with nature. Stoics recognize that everything that happens is part of the natural order of the universe and is therefore worthy of acceptance and love. By aligning themselves with the rhythms of nature and embracing their fate, Stoics can find meaning, purpose, and serenity in every moment of their lives.

Negative visualization

Negative visualization involves intentionally imagining the loss or absence of things we often take for granted, such as loved ones, possessions, health, or status. By vividly envisioning the absence of these elements in our lives, we come to appreciate their true value and deepen our sense of gratitude for their presence.

It also serves to counteract hedonic adaptation—the tendency to become accustomed to positive circumstances and take them for granted over time. By periodically contemplating the loss of these circumstances, we prevent ourselves from becoming complacent or entitled, allowing us to derive greater satisfaction and meaning from our lives.

Books about stoicism

1. "How to be a stoic" by M. Pigliucci

If you've ever been curious about Stoicism and how it can help us navigate life's challenges with clarity and resilience, this is the perfect place to start. In the book, Pigliucci explores the core principles of Stoicism and offers practical tips and exercises for applying its wisdom to our everyday lives. Whether you're seeking greater meaning, struggling with your emotions, or simply interested in personal growth, Stoicism has something valuable to offer. I found the book to be incredibly insightful and accessible, and I consider it to be incredibly relevant to modern life. A good book for a beginner, as it covers all of the topics of stoicism, and explains them in a simple manner.

2. "Letters from a stoic" by Seneca

Seneca was a prominent Roman Stoic philosopher and statesman who lived during the first century AD. His letters were written to a friend named Lucilius and reflect the political, social, and cultural milieu of ancient Rome. The book consists of a series of letters written by Seneca to Lucilius. Each letter addresses a specific topic or theme related to Stoic philosophy and offers Seneca's insights and advice on how to live a virtuous life.

3. "Enchiridion" by Epictetus

"Enchiridion" is a manual of Stoic ethical advice attributed to the Stoic philosopher Epictetus. He was born a slave in Hierapolis, Phrygia (present-day Turkey), around 55 AD, and later became a prominent teacher of Stoicism in Rome. It is written in a concise and aphoristic style, presenting Stoic teachings in short, easily digestible passages. Each passage addresses a specific aspect of Stoic ethics and offers practical advice for living a virtuous life.

4. "Meditations" by Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius was a Roman Emperor who ruled from 161 to 180 AD. "Meditations" is a collection of aphorisms and journal entries written by Marcus Aurelius primarily for his own personal reflection and self-improvement. The text is divided into twelve books, each addressing different themes such as the nature of the self, the pursuit of virtue, and the acceptance of fate.

5. "The Daily Stoic 366" by R. Holiday and S. Hanselman

"The Daily Stoic" is designed as a daily devotional, with 366 short passages—one for each day of the year. Each passage includes a quote from a Stoic philosopher, followed by commentary and reflection by Holiday and Hanselman. The book is structured to provide daily inspiration and guidance for applying Stoic principles to modern life.
